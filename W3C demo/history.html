Founding

Founded on October 1st 1994 by Tim Berners-Lee, the W3C is a collaboration between the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) and the European Organization for Nuclear Research (CERN). The project was jointly supported by the European Union and the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA), a division of the United States Department of Defense.

Today the W3C is jointly hosted by MIT's Laboratory for Computer Science in the United States, the Institut National de Recherche en Information et en Automatique (INRIA) in France, and Keio University in Japan.

Tim Berners-Lee's ambitions for the Web, the founding philosophies behind it and the formation of the W3C, are to make the Web open and accessible for everyone. This reflects the main ambition of the W3C; the standardisation and continual advancement of the Web. This means the coding and implementation of the Web. But what it also means is the underlying philosophy that any individual regardless of difference in age, culture, resources, education or abilities has the opportunity to be involved.

Timeline

  • 1980 - Tim Berners-Lee writes the 'ENQUIRE' system, a pre-cursor to the modern Web
    • ENQUIRE introduces the concept of making links between arbitrary nodes.
    • Although ENQUIRE uses hypertext, the system is very restrictive and limited by technical capabilities and expertise.
    • Tim Berners-Lee wants to develop a system that is open for everyone.
  • 1990 - Development of World Wide Web (WWW) begins. First Web client-server communication over the Internet later that year.
  • 1993 - European Organisation for Nuclear Research (CERN) releases Web protocol and code allowing anybody to use it royalty free.
  • 1994 - World Wide Web Consortium is founded by Tim Berners-Lee, Al Vezza and Michael Dertouzous.
  • 1997 - First HTML recommendation published by the W3C - HTML 3.2.
  • 1998 - Guidelines for content internationalisation are adopted by W3C.
